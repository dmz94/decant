Not-for-profit Mayo Clinic reports record profit last year

Rochester-based health system saw more patient demand and growing philanthropy last year, though it also closed clinics in southern Minnesota.

By Christopher Snowbeck

The Minnesota Star Tribune
March 6, 2026 at 4:27PM
The Mayo Clinic's Gonda Building in Rochester in 2014. Mayo reported another record year for profits in 2025. (Glen Stubbe/The Minnesota Star Tribune)
Advertisement

Mayo Clinic reported record profits last year driven by strong patient demand for health care services, big investment returns and more philanthropic giving to the Rochester-based health system.

Financial statements show the not-for-profit health care provider collected more than $21 billion in revenue from medical care, grants and investments/philanthropy, while paying out about $20 billion in expenses. That left $1.47 billion in earnings from current activities — the biggest one-year sum ever at the clinic.

Mayo’s financial stability comes as it closed half a dozen clinics in Minnesota last year and stands in contrast to other health systems in the state. Minnesota’s largest safety-net hospital in Minneapolis is clamoring for financial help and medical centers more broadly are bracing for substantial cuts in Medicaid next year.

After factoring in gifts from donors that weren’t available for activities in 2025 plus unallocated investment income, Mayo’s net assets grew by a record $4.55 billion.

Dennis Dahlen, Mayo’s chief financial officer, called the financial performance “timely” as work continues on a $5 billion expansion project in Rochester and $2 billion worth of capital investments at Mayo’s large medical center in Phoenix.

“Those construction project commitments were a bit of a stretch for us,” Dahlen said. The funding also allowed for a 20% expansion in clinical trials last year, he said.

Hospital admissions last year climbed nearly 7% compared with 2024, while surgical and outpatient cases grew by more than 4% across Mayo’s operations, according to the financial materials released this week.

“We saw more patients than we had planned, and significantly more than we did see the prior year,” Dahlen said. “We had a great year — and the nation had a great year — in investment returns. And we also saw a really strong increase in philanthropy.”

“It’s in the unusual convergence of those three things.”

By the end of last year, double-digit percent growth on the clinic’s massive investment portfolio helped push the value of Mayo’s endowment to $7.9 billion. Along the way, donors in 2025 provided nearly $1 billion in gifts to the clinic.

Mayo’s financial position allows for “introducing new cures, new tests, new diagnostics, better things — we’ve got almost 500 AI algorithms and bots and tools in clinical practice, or very near to that“ Dahlen said. ”Those developments require as much, if not more, sustained development than a construction project building bricks-and-mortar space for the next generation of patients.”

Mayo runs large hospital campuses in Arizona, Florida and Minnesota, as well as the Mayo Clinic Health System network of community hospitals and clinics primarily in Minnesota and Wisconsin.

Last year Mayo announced the closures of six community medical clinics across southern Minnesota, sparking criticism from community groups concerned about local access to health care. Mayo said the closures were necessary because of low staffing and patient volumes.

As a nonprofit, Mayo doesn’t have shareholders to receive earnings. Instead, the funds are reinvested in Mayo’s patient care operations, Medical School and research enterprise.

The Minnesota Star Tribune uses the term “profit” at groups like Mayo nonetheless to reflect the significant capacity for the state’s largest health care nonprofits to take in more money than they spend.

Profits from what the clinic calls “current activities” — meaning health care services plus a portion of Mayo’s investment and philanthropic funding available for work in 2025 — came in at $1.47 billion last year, a 13.5% increase over operating income of $1.3 billion during 2024.

Mayo’s income from current activities in each of the last two years was a record in terms of sheer volume of dollars. As a percentage of revenue, Dahlen said, earnings were larger at certain times many years ago, when Mayo was a smaller organization.

Net assets, which includes all investment returns and philanthropic giving last year, grew by more than 50%, to $4.55 billion.

Excluding all investment and philanthropy funds, the clinic’s core health care business generated $473 million in operating income during 2025, up 72% from the previous year. Profit at the core health care business was about 2.3% of revenue, a margin that’s more comparable to financial results generated at many other health systems.

Put another way, investments and philanthropy are a significant part of what makes Mayo Clinic stronger in financial terms than many other operators of hospitals and clinics.

Current numbers for the in-state workforce weren’t available, but overall employment grew from 83,000 to 85,000.

Other performance details for 2025 include:

  • Mayo provided care to patients from every state and 141 countries;
    • The clinic performed 149 new surgeries and procedures in 2025, while implementing more than 100 new tests at its lab division;
      • Mayo performed more than 2,000 solid organ transplants and nearly 800 bone marrow transplants;
        • Retail pharmacies operated by Mayo saw revenue increase by 24%, to $819 million;
          • Even Mayo cafeterias saw growth, reporting total sales of $46 million, a 12% increase.
