Tested: 2026 Land Rover Discovery Is an Eager Beaver

The Disco's turbocharged 3.0-liter belies an otherwise chill experience.

Published: Mar 11, 2026
Reviewed by Andrew KrokManaging Editor, Reviews
View exterior photos

The Land Rover Discovery has been chugging along in its current generation for nearly a decade, and its most recent refresh took place when social distancing was still the norm. Indeed, that feels like a lifetime ago, and several newer competitors have since stolen some of the Discovery's moxie. But just like the music genre, the Disco doesn't appear to be going anywhere, so let's see how it feels in its modern context.

The 2026 model year brings two new trims: Gemini and Tempest. They occupy the top two spots of this four-tier lineup and represent the only way to get Land Rover's more powerful engine option, a turbocharged 3.0-liter inline-six making 355 horsepower and 369 pound-feet of torque with 48-volt hybrid assist. A 296-hp turbo four is offered on the two least expensive models, but considering that the Gemini crossed our scales at a chunky 5671 pounds, you'll want the most motivation possible under your right foot. Sure, the four-cylinder probably weighs less (we haven't tested one), but not 1000 pounds less. All variants include standard four-wheel drive and an eight-speed automatic transmission.

At the test track, the Discovery Gemini made it to 60 mph in a decent 6.6 seconds, which is 0.4 second behind the last Lexus GX we tested and 0.7 second behind the Audi Q7, both of which also pack six-cylinder engines. (The GX weighs about one human less than the Disco, and the Q7 was a whopping 540 pounds lighter on our scales.) The Land Rover's powertrain isn't the most responsive, as evidenced by its 7.3-second run from 5 to 60 mph. The delta between these standing-start and rolling-start times was a tenth greater than what we saw from both the GX and the Q7.

view exterior photos

The Discovery's powertrain wasn't exactly a darling on the road, either. The throttle is missing a sweet spot. On the low end, if you breathe on the right pedal, you'll be a little pokier than the cars around you. But if you dare move the pedal an extra few microns before leaving first gear, the engine will quite literally leap into action, catapulting you forward and sending the tach needle into low earth orbit. The solution, which doesn't really feel natural, is to apply gentle throttle through first gear and then slowly dial in more after the transmission shifts into second.

Thankfully, the brake pedal doesn't have that problem; it's quite easy to modulate for smooth stops every time. Stopping from 70 mph required 164 feet at the test track, eight feet better than the 172-foot Lexus GX and 11 feet ahead of the Audi Q7.

view interior Photos

The Discovery is not an astute handler. It can get a little wallowy when the going gets lateral. However, its 285/40R-22 Pirelli Scorpion Zero All Season tires helped it achieve a 300-foot skidpad result of 0.82 g, just a bit behind the Q7 (0.86 g) but leagues ahead of the even more discombobulated Lexus GX (0.75 g). That said, in steady-state operation, the Discovery and its air springs gave us a smooth, comfortable ride that clearly prioritizes family comfort—except in the tight third row, where adults will not have a good time for very long.

HIGHS: Comfortable over the road, quality interior materials, can rove all sorts of lands.

Get the Disco into open air on the expressway, and you'll find so-so fuel economy. Our 75-mph highway fuel-economy test delivered a 22-mpg result, 1 mpg below the EPA estimate. That's also 1 mpg higher than our result from the boxier Lexus GX, which matched its 21-mpg EPA highway rating. The Land Rover is also the louder than its competitors when cruising at 70 mph; its 67-decibel result is one higher than the GX and two higher than the Q7.

view interior Photos

Let's talk about the cabin a bit more. As with Land Rover's other (and also expensive) products, it's a nice place to burn some time. The layout is clean—maybe too clean, as the area around the finicky gear lever feels a little empty, but there's something to be said about minimalism when many higher-end automakers are determined to shove ten pounds of flashy crap into a five-pound sack. Just about every touchpoint feels nice. Other than that kids-only third row, which noticeably impinges upon cargo space when extended, there's very little to complain about. Visibility is good, even rearward, but only when the middle headrests are stowed. The space under the center armrest is reserved for a refrigerator, which you may or may not appreciate.

LOWS: Jumpy six-cylinder powertrain, so-so fuel economy, luggage space disappears when all seats in use.

The Discovery's tech stack is fine. The Pivi Pro infotainment system has a nice, clean design, though some of the functions can be a little tricky to find the first time around. Thankfully, committing these moves to muscle memory is easy, and the 11.4-inch touchscreen is bright. The 12.3-inch gauge cluster, on the other hand, feels old enough to vote; the middle portion and the way it displays data have been standardized since the F-type (R.I.P.).

view interior Photos

Our test Disco came loaded with all manner of options, ballooning its $72,650 base price to $83,195, nearly as much as the top-tier trim. Thankfully, there are a few bucks to save along the way here. Unless you plan on using the Land Rover to the extent of its name, you can ditch the $2400 Advanced Off-Road pack, which adds a two-speed transfer case, a rear locking differential, and other safari-adjacent goodies. If you have enough muscle mass to move a seat, the $700 electronic folding third row can take a hike. The $1650 22-inch wheels are only for vanity, and the $1100 tow hitch receiver is obviously unnecessary if you never tow. (If you do, the Disco can tug a notable 8200 pounds.) The $1400 combination of Wi-Fi and a head-up display isn't mandatory either. But we'd stick with the $955 Cold Climate package, which adds heated second-row seats, a heated windshield, heated windshield-wiper jets, and power washers for the headlights.

view exterior photos

Still, the six-cylinder Discovery represents a pretty high cost of entry for a vehicle rubbing up against the door of the 10-Year Club. It's a comfortable cruiser with off-road chops, but in the time since its birth, plenty of other similar (but potentially more interesting) fish have entered the sea.

VERDICT: Disco isn’t dead, it’s holding steady.
